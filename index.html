+ About

Hi! I'm interested in people and computers.

I studied at the University of Chicago from 2016 to 2020, double majoring in computer science and sociology. Academically, I pursued a specialization in computer systems and a BA Thesis on gender roles in an online game. Outside the classroom, I've gotten a taste of game design as well as nonprofit community development in Chicago, and of teaching English in Korea. I've also had fun building projects, including some simply for my own curiosity. I'd love to keep doing technically involved and socially meaningful work.

Computer Science
Advanced Distributed Systems (Ian Foster) Discussed significant papers from ~1980s to present. Data Visualization Mapping data to dimensions in graphs and how people view visualizations. Using Node.js to create images from numbers.
Honors Theory of Algorithms™ (Laszlo Babai) Proving correctness and lower and upper bounds on complexity. Some dynamic programming, a lot of graph theory. NP completeness and Karp reductions. In order to cover a lot of material, Babai assigns many "Do" exercises during lecture, which are to check your understanding rather than hand in. This frees him from the burden of explaining or grading which typically limits how much material can be taught in a course, allowing him to cover more. Also, he will fight you over where you put each upside down A and reverse E.
Introduction to Computer Systems Before 154 I thought of a computer as something you can use to do interesting things, but then the idea of this class was that the computer itself is interesting (actually, the real idea was to attack you with C projects but that doesn't sound as nice).
Introduction to Databases Relational algebra, SQL, ER diagrams. In terms of systems, trade offs around io costs for ways of storing pages in memory and on disk and indexing records. Implemented a "simple" relational database in Java.
Foundations of Computer Networks (Borja Sotomayor) Mostly layers 2-4: Ethernet, WiFi, a bit on switches and access points, IP, TCP, DNS. Implemented core sections of IRC and TCP and a routing table in C.
Parallel Computing (Hank Hoffman) Some theory problems and reasoning in a parallel environment, mainly implementing various locks and parallel data structures/functions in C.
Sociology
Critical Videogame Studies (Patrick Jagoda) Useful theoretical background for my BA thesis. Prototyped a broken but cool post apocalyptic story game. Because everyone in class had a shared vocabulary for discussing games from readings and lectures, there were a lot of interesting blog posts. I had one good post but I'd recommend checking out others like Peter Forberg and Samantha Xiao. As opposed to my posts (long winded spiels I wouldn't subject my worst enemy to) theirs are concise, funny, and thoughtful.

Ethnographic Field Methods Theory and practice of choosing a site, gaining access, making observations, choosing questions, writing field notes, and putting it into a paper. I did the class ethnography/paper/presentation on a gaming cafe in Chicago as practice for my thesis.
Mathematical Sociology Mainly about ways of analyzing networks of people. Used Facebook friends in final paper to show students in Alper House are more connected than those in Vue53.
Sociology of Deviant Behavior™ (Kristen Schilt) Deviance, obviously, but also normal behavior and the boundary work in their relationship. I really liked someone named, believe it or not, David Matza. In my final paper I wrote about how a flamer in League of Legends uses his neutralization techniques.
Sociology of Higher Education (Melissa Osborne) How student background influences navigating college, and college as a sieve, incubator, temple, and hub. It was a cold winter (polar vortex!) and I wrote a fun paper on Canada Goose as cultural capital.
Sociology Theory™ (Kimberly Hoang) Fairly reading and writing intensive but also intense lectures under threat of random popsicle stick cold call. Statistical Models and Methods Probability distribution functions, random variables and expected values. Correlation, confidence intervals, and linear regression. R in RStudio for calculations and displaying descriptive statistics and graphs.

+ Employment

Some highlights from my recent work making games with Game Changer Chicago, building community networks with All Stars Project of Chicago, and teaching English with a private school in Korea, including a few pictures:


working with two other interns

Interning at the Game Changer Chicago Design Lab, I prototyped a dating sim game intended to dissuade incoming college freshman from abusing alcohol and/or sexually harassing others. I felt a game is a more engaging alternative to the modules incoming students click through, often without paying much attention. The project entailed surverying literature, creating art, writing, character stories and dialogue, coding minigames, and actually putting the prototype together. This is a lot! Fortunately, I worked with eight other (fantastic) interns so I focused on the last two tasks, mainly working in JavaScript.


To coordinate multiple teams, we held brief meetings and stuck sticky notes on the wall to organize our progress and goals. As a result of sharing recent work and expected deadlines, we ended up creating minigames based on stories, and vice versa. For example, as I was told we had a scene in a bar, I incorporated a drink mixing minigame to convey factors determining safe alcohol consumption. In the opposite direction, I wanted to create a rhythm game, which inspired others to write an arcade scene. The individual pieces were modular enough that nobody needed to know the inner workings of what others were doing, yet we were able to draw inspiration from one another’s work.


I also got to play a bunch more games. Some of these were fun; others were broken and needed much more playtesting. Furthermore, I had to deal with the tension between making the sort of gameplay that would be satisfying for me personally and adapting the game to its intended audience.

My work at the All Stars Project of Chicago was roughly split between producing events to connect Chicagoland and provide opportunities to youth in underserved communities and the administrative side of a nonprofit.


In the office, I mainly helped organize people and materials for upcoming events. This was at times the more boring side. To save myself from manually updating hundreds and hundreds of people in a database of contacts from their LinkedIn information, I automated the process of looking them up and getting their information.


The two main programs I worked on were a talent show to develop community and an internship preparation and placement program for youth in the South and West Sides of Chicago with companies in the Loop. All Stars facilitates interactions between people from all across Chicago, including a pathway to corporations disadvantaged youth have relatively little access to. Forging this path to corporations for youth meant doing outreach in both boardrooms and neighborhoods.


students working in groups

students choosing Hangman word

I quickly abandoned my initial ambitious but unrealistic ideas (what if we sang Hamilton?!) after my first time teaching a dozen Korean 13 year olds. I learned to create lesson plans and tune the content and difficulty of vocabulary words to my two classes of students, as one was slightly older and more advanced. I next implemented practicable exercises such as crossword puzzles, then started playing games such as hangman, rewarding victorious students with mango and pineapple chewy candies from the grocery store across from the Hagwon.


Competitive exercises and games suit young Korean students striving to improve and be the best. By the end of the summer, I tried a game where all students stood up in a circle and passed around an eraser, and, when it was their turn, had to say an English vocabulary word or sit down. With all eyes on one of the last two remaining standing students struggling desperately to come up with a word, the rest chanted “TEN, NINE, EIGHT, SEVEN…”


When I gave the class the English vocabulary sheets back and told them they had one minute to study in preparation for the next round, their intense focus was gratifying both in terms of their English language learning and my growth as a teacher. Some read so intently they were literally physically quivering. When I asked for the papers back, students hid them under the table to snatch an extra ten seconds with the words. I had to stop them from writing on their own skin. I do not want kids writing on their skin, but I like that it is a conceivable idea because everyone in the classroom is wholeheartedly motivated.
