Selected Courses

Tastiest Bites of the Smorgasbord

Sometimes I see "selected" such and such and I’m like what does "selected" mean? So, to be clear, my selection criteria here is this class was cool or provocative or something to the extent that I’d enjoy discussing it. It follows I'd be happy to talk about any of these at davidmatz@uchicago.edu :)

I included the instructor's name where I felt nobody else would teach the class the same. UChicago talks a lot about Rigor™ so I put a ™ on classes I felt were Rigor™. I put numbers for core sequences over multiple quarters.

Core
America in World Civ (1-2-3) Imperial influences, Civil War, and human rights.
Classics of Social and Political Thought (1-2-3) (Emma Saunders-Hastings) Real order: classical, political, and social thought.
Honors Intro to Calculus (1-2-3) Proving everything from the ground up.
Honors Intro to Computer Science (1-2) Haskell is trying too hard. C isn't trying hard enough.
Philosophical Perspectives (1-2-3) (Ben Callard) Edgy takes can be fun, but beware that kid.
Computer Science
Computer Architecture™ Implemented a multi stage, branch predicting, cache simulating ARM instruction pipeline in C. We went over caches, but also spent a while on topologies of processor networks.
Computers for Learning (Diana Franklin) Lectures and readings on technology in pedagogy. Made a Java game for English language learners. I felt some topics had good nuance, or at least a good direction of research, including ordering CS prerequisites and engaging students in virtual classrooms. But sometimes I felt STEM people just recently realized drawing circuits on the blackboard with your back turned to students makes for ineffective teaching, and reacted by ditching the idea of a good lecture for active learning. Taking notes in a lecture is learning actively too.
Data Visualization Mapping data to dimensions in graphs and how people view visualizations. Using Node.js to create images from numbers.
Honors Theory of Algorithms™ (Laszlo Babai) Proving algorithm correctness and asymptotic complexity. Some dynamic programming, a lot of graph theory. NP completeness and Karp reductions. In order to cover a lot of material, Babai assigns many "Do" exercises during lecture, which are to check your understanding rather than hand in. This frees him from the burden of explaining or grading which typically bounds how much material can be covered in a course, allowing him to cover a lot. Also, he will fight you over where you put each upside down A and reverse E.
Introduction to Computer Systems Before 154 I thought of a computer as something you can use to do interesting things, but then the idea of this class was that the computer itself is interesting (actually, the real idea was to attack you with C projects but that doesn't sound as nice).
Introduction to Databases (Aaron Elmore) Relational algebra makes sense and is cool and maybe even useful for SQL or whatever. ER diagrams are somewhere between unknowable chaos and satanic scrawls. Anyways, on the systems side, trade offs around io costs for ways of storing pages in memory and on disk and indexing records. Implemented a "simple" relational database in Java.
Foundations of Computer Networks (Borja Sotomayor) Mostly layers 2-4: Ethernet, WiFi, a bit on switches and access points, IP, TCP, DNS. Implemented core sections of IRC and TCP and a routing table in C. The material could omit needless words and include more useful ones, imo.
Parallel Computing (Hank Hoffman) im putting this up while in the class. u might even say, in parallel. haha. ill leave now
Sociology
Critical Videogame Studies (Patrick Jagoda) Really a CMST class, but related to my thesis so yeah. Wrote an insane paper arguing Nietzsche reads Mario > Braid > Problem Attic as the slave revolt in morality and Mario is his redeemer. Prototyped a broken but cool post apocalyptic story game. Because everyone in class had a shared vocabulary for discussing games from readings and lectures, there were a lot of interesting blog posts. I had one good post but I'd really really really recommend checking out others like Peter Forberg and Samantha Xiao. As opposed to my posts (long winded spiels I wouldn't subject my worst enemy to) theirs are concise, funny, and thoughtful.
Effects of Schooling A survey of the statistical health, wealth, and happiness benefits of schooling.
Ethnographic Field Methods Theory and practice of choosing a site, gaining access, making observations, choosing questions, writing field notes, and putting it into a paper. I did the quarter long ethnography/final paper/class presentation on a gaming cafe in Chicago as a practice run for my thesis.
Mathematical Sociology Mainly about ways of analyzing networks of people. In my final paper I used Facebook friends to represent connections in Alper and Vue53, two UChicago houses. Various measures show Alper is the better connected house, which fits with Vue53's position as not really a real house.
Sociology of Deviant Behavior™ (Kristen Schilt) Deviance, obviously, but also normal behavior and the boundary work in their relationship. I really liked someone named, believe it or not, David Matza. In my final paper I wrote about how a flamer in League uses his neutralization techniques.
Sociology of Higher Education (Melissa Osborne) How students navigate college depending on their background, and comparing college as a sieve, incubator, temple, and hub. It was a cold winter (polar vortex!) and I wrote a fun paper on Canada Goose as cultural capital.
Sociology Theory™ (Kimberly Hoang) Fairly reading and writing intensive but also intense lectures under threat of random popsicle stick cold call. In some ways the standard Marx Weber Durkheim up through recent theorists, but also a broader range of viewpoints and contextualization of their lives and goals, and applying everything to American Factory. From the weird personal reflection/applying theory final paper, I'd like to share my personal issue with a lot of social science research on League.
Statistical Models and Methods Probability distribution functions, random variables and expected values. Correlation, confidence intervals, and linear regression. R in RStudio for calculations and displaying descriptive statistics and graphs.

Hi! I'm interested in people and computers. Obviously, this encompasses quite a bit.

To narrow it down, I'm talking about the intersection like peanut butter and chocolate: cool on their own, but together...

As an example, I'm currently writing a thesis arguing, based on survey and interview data, that players in enact gender roles in League of Legends, an ostensibly anonymous, online context, using meanings developed by the game's community.

Let me clarify that this is not exactly HCI or developing new technology for interfacing between humans and machines. That might be related and is all well and good, I'm just interested in particular ways people interact with one another, online.
